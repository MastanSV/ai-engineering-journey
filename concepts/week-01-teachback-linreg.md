## What problem does linear regression solve?

Linear regression solves some problem that is why we are learning this. the problem, let say you have some 10 friends, you know their salary and years of experience they have and their salaries and experience has wide range. you listed them on paper, and you want to know the salary of person (who is not your friend) but you know their experience. This is exactly what the linear regression problem does. How the linear regression does this is by like, it finds the simplest relationship (commonly straight line) between the salary and experience, and it lets you predict the salary based on the experience.

## The line: y = wx + b

In the above paragraph, i have said that, linear regression finds the simplest relationship i.e., straight line, and it has formula: y = w x + b, don't be afraid, here x you consider the experience and y can be considered salary, we are considering salary on y because the target variable nothing but what we want to predict has to be considered on y. and we left out with w and b, these are very important in predicting the salary based on the experience. the term w - is called slope, in general terms like w we got is 1000 it means that if the experience is increased by 1 year then the salary will be increased by 1000. b in technical terms it is called as intercept it means that this salary and experience like, if suppose the person has 0 years of experience then what will be his salary. that is recorded in the term b. Now, you know what will be the key knobs plays role in predicting the salary. one is w and other is b

## How does the model know which line is good? (MSE)

So till now we understand what linear regression solves and how do we predict the values and the equation for that, so let's consider that we have predicted the relationship i.e., straight line and how does the model knows which line is good for linear regression to predict the values? for that we have MSE, Mean Squared Error, which tells that what is the error we have for the line, so what the model will do is it will find the best line which has less MSE, so now question will be how do you calculate the MSE, right? so lets take example and understand, suppose from the data you know, consider 5 years of experience guy is getting salary of 3L but as per the line which we drawn, 5 years of experience guy is getting 2L salary. now 1L is the difference in salary. Let's consider the another example, from the data we know, for 8 years of experience guy is getting 6L and from the line, 8 years of experience guy is getting 8L, the difference is 2L. so first point we have difference 1L and for second point we have -2L, so in MSE we square them 1 + 4 = 5L and divide by number of samples we took, for this example we take 2 points so it will be 5L/2 = 2.5L, like this MSE will be calculated for all the lines and the line which has least error will considered as best line for the data.
The reason for squaring is that if the error is big it will square and it makes very big and if the error is small and if we square error is small only there will be negligible difference. Squaring punish big errors very big which helps in finding the best line and correct MSE. So MSE will be low for best line that suits the data.

## Bias vs variance — the Goldilocks problem

Now we have the relationship line for the data is ready, now because this straight line is too rigid it might not connect the all the data points, which is called bias, so straight line will have high bias. and consider that if the relationship line we are making is not straight line, its a kind squiggle line which connects the all datapoints, and we call it has low bias, but when you change to the another dataset this squiggle line wont be connecting new data points in new data set which is called high variance. but consider we have straight line which has low variance. So the correct dataline will just learns the real pattern and ignores the noise. So linear regression strictly sits with straight line with high bias and low variance, sometimes that is enough and sometimes we need curvy line for that we need tools.

"A straight line is too rigid — it can't capture every twist in the data, so it's _systematically wrong_ in places. We call this **high bias** — like a stubborn friend who has one idea about how the world works.

The opposite extreme is a squiggly line that hugs every data point perfectly. Zero error on the training data — but the moment you give it new data, it falls apart, because it memorized the noise instead of learning the pattern. That's **high variance** — the model swings wildly depending on what data it saw.

The trick is finding the middle: a model flexible enough to catch the real pattern, simple enough to ignore the noise. Linear regression sits firmly on the high-bias/low-variance side — sometimes that's exactly right, sometimes life is curvy and you need a stronger tool."
